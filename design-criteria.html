What are design criteria?
Definition
Design criteria are the rules your design must follow. They spell out exactly what your solution needs to do, who it is for, and how you will know if it actually works.
When do you write them?
You write design criteria before you start designing, based on what you have learned from your research, your target audience, or your client.
The golden rule: every criterion must be specific and measurable. If you cannot test whether your design meets it, it is not a good criterion.
Worked Example
Vague vs Specific
I Do
Both of these are attempts at a design criterion. Only one of them is actually useful.
Vague criterion
"The product must be strong."
What does "strong" mean here? Strong enough to hold what? There is no way to test this.
→
Specific criterion
"The structure must support a static load of 500g without visible deformation."
Clear. Testable. You know exactly what to measure and what counts as passing.
Ask yourself: could two different people test this criterion independently and get the same result? If not, it needs to be more specific.
Good or Vague?
We Do
Hold up 1 finger = good  |  2 fingers = vague. Be ready to explain why and suggest how to fix the vague ones.
Criterion A
"The design must look nice and be appealing to the target audience."
Vague. "Nice" cannot be measured. Better: "The design must use a maximum of three colours selected from the client's brand guidelines."
Criterion B
"The product must fit inside a standard school locker (30cm × 45cm when flat)."
Good. Clear dimensions. Testable with a ruler right now.
Criterion C
"The design must be eco-friendly and sustainable."
Vague. Better: "At least 50% of materials must be recycled, upcycled, or from renewable suppliers."
Criterion D
"A first-time user must complete the main task in under 60 seconds without instructions."
Good. Specific time, specific condition, independently testable.

Criteria Categories
Categories help you cover all the angles of your design. You do not have to use every one, but they are a useful thinking prompt.
Function: How well does it work?
"Must hold items up to 2kg without tearing."
Aesthetic: How does it look?
"Must use a cohesive colour scheme of no more than three colours."
Sustainability: What is the environmental impact?
"Must use at least 80% recycled or second-hand materials."
User Need: Does it suit the user?
"Must be lightweight enough for daily carrying (under 400g when empty)."
Requirement: at least one of your five criteria must relate to sustainability.
Let’s write together
We Do
Scenario
A netball coach runs sessions at three different courts each week. They travel by public transport and need a solution to carry equipment: training bibs, cones, a first aid kit, water bottles, and a clipboard.
For each category: write one criterion on your whiteboard. Make it specific and measurable. Chin it when you are done.
1
Function: What must it do? Think about load, access, and movement.
2
Aesthetic: How should it look in a sporting context?
3
Sustainability: How do the materials or production reduce environmental impact?
4
User Need: What does this person's routine demand? Think: public transport, multiple venues.
Is it specific enough to test? Could a stranger read it and know exactly what to measure?
