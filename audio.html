CHUNK 1: UNDERSTANDING SOUND WAVES

TEACHER CHUNK
8 mins

How Does Sound Work in the Real World?

Sound in real life is a wave that travels through the air - like ripples in a pond! When you speak or play music, you create vibrations that make the air move up and down in waves.

1

Sound as a Wave

Here's what a sound wave looks like in the real world:

Sound waves are created by vibrating objects pushing air molecules back and forth:

  1. When something vibrates (like a guitar string), it pushes nearby air molecules
  2. These molecules bump into their neighbors, creating a chain reaction
  3. This creates areas of high pressure (compression) and low pressure (rarefaction)
  4. These pressure changes travel through the air as waves
  5. When they reach your ear, your eardrum vibrates in the same pattern!
2

The Computer's Challenge

Computers can't understand continuous waves directly - they only understand numbers (specifically, 1s and 0s, called binary).

Sound Wave

Continuous, smooth changes in air pressure over time.

Computer Data

Discrete 1s and 0s (binary digits) that can only represent specific values.

So we need a way to convert smooth, continuous sound waves into specific numbers a computer can store and process!

3

Two Key Conversions

We convert sound waves to binary data using two important processes:

Sampling Frequency

Taking many quick snapshots of the wave at regular intervals (like taking photos of a moving object).

Bit Depth

Measuring how tall the wave is at each snapshot with specific precision (like measuring with different sized rulers).


Key Points

SOUND IS A WAVE

Sound travels through air as pressure waves created by vibrating objects

COMPUTERS USE BINARY

Computers can only process discrete numbers (1s and 0s), not continuous waves

CONVERSION IS KEY

We use sampling frequency and bit depth to convert waves to binary data

CHUNK 2: SAMPLING FREQUENCY

TEACHER CHUNK
10 mins

Taking Snapshots of Sound

To convert a continuous sound wave into digital data, we first need to take many snapshots of the wave at regular intervals. This is called sampling.

1

What is Sampling?

Sampling is like taking photos of a moving object. The more photos you take per second, the smoother the movement appears when you play them back!

Sampling Rate is the number of snapshots (samples) we take per second, measured in Hertz (Hz).

2

Sampling Demo

See how different sampling rates affect how accurately we capture a sound wave:

What's happening: The blue line is the original smooth sound wave. The red dots show where we're taking samples. With fewer samples, we miss many details of the wave!

3

Sampling in the Real World

Different applications use different sampling rates depending on the audio quality needed:

Application Sampling Rate Quality
Telephone Calls 8,000 Hz (8 kHz) Speech only, no music
AM Radio 22,050 Hz (22.05 kHz) Basic music quality
CD Audio 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz) Full range music
Professional Audio 96,000 Hz (96 kHz) or higher Studio recording, film

The Nyquist Theorem: To accurately capture a sound of frequency X, you need a sampling rate of at least 2X. That's why CD audio at 44.1 kHz can capture frequencies up to 22.05 kHz (slightly above human hearing range).

CHUNK 3: BIT DEPTH

TEACHER CHUNK
10 mins

Measuring the Height of Sound Waves

After we decide when to take samples (sampling rate), we need to decide how precisely to measure each sample. This is called bit depth.

1

What is Bit Depth?

Bit depth determines how precisely we can measure the height (amplitude) of each sample. More bits means we can represent more different volume levels!

Low Bit Depth

Few possible volume levels

High Bit Depth

Many possible volume levels

Formula: With n bits of bit depth, you can represent 2^n different volume levels.

2

Bit Depth Demo

See how different bit depths affect how smoothly we can represent a sound wave:

What's happening: The blue line is the original smooth wave. The red line shows how we represent it digitally with limited bit depth. With lower bit depth, we get a "stair-step" pattern instead of a smooth curve.

3

Bit Depth in the Real World

Different applications use different bit depths depending on the audio quality needed:

Bit Depth Volume Levels Common Uses
8-bit 256 levels Telephone, older games
16-bit 65,536 levels CDs, standard digital audio
24-bit 16.7 million levels Professional audio, studio recording
32-bit float 4.2 billion+ levels High-end audio production

Key concept: Low bit depth creates "quantization error" - the stair-step effect you see in the demo. This manifests as noise in the audio, especially in quiet passages.

2

File Size Calculations

The quality of digital audio directly affects its file size. Here's how to calculate it:

Audio File Size Calculation

For uncompressed audio (like WAV files):

File Size = (Sample Rate × Bit Depth × Duration × Channels) ÷ 8

The result is in bytes. Divide by 8 to convert from bits to bytes.

Example: 3-minute stereo song at CD quality

Sample Rate = 44,100 Hz
Bit Depth = 16 bits
Duration = 3 minutes = 180 seconds
Channels = 2 (stereo)

File Size = (44,100 × 16 × 180 × 2) ÷ 8
File Size = 31,752,000 bytes
File Size ≈ 30.3 MB
3

Compressed Audio

Most digital audio we use is compressed to save space. Compression removes some information to make files smaller.

MP3

Popular compressed format that removes sounds humans can't easily hear

Can be 10x smaller than WAV files

AAC

Used by Apple Music, YouTube, and many streaming services

Better quality than MP3 at the same file size

Bitrate is how we measure compressed audio quality - like 128 kbps (kilobits per second) or 320 kbps. Higher bitrate = higher quality but larger file size.

Lesson Summary

1

Sound Waves to Binary

Sound waves are continuous patterns of pressure in the air. Computers convert these into binary data using sampling and bit depth.

2

Sampling Frequency

Sampling rate determines how many times per second we measure the wave's height. Higher sampling rates capture higher frequencies and more detail.

3

Bit Depth

Bit depth determines how precisely we measure the wave's height at each sample. Higher bit depth means more volume levels and smoother audio.

4

File Size vs. Quality

Higher quality audio (higher sampling rate and bit depth) results in larger file sizes. We use compression to reduce file size while maintaining acceptable quality.
