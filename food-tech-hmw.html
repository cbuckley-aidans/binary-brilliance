An uncomfortable question.
Cancer rates are generally declining in older Australians ‚Äî better screening, better treatment. But for people under 50, one specific cancer is trending in the wrong direction.
Turn and talk: which form of cancer do you think is rising fastest among young people?
Make an estimate ‚Äî then we'll see who's right.
It's bowel cancer.
Colorectal (bowel) cancer is Australia's second most common cancer. Rates in adults over 50 have been slowly declining. In young people, the trend is the opposite.
Rising rates
Colorectal cancer in Australians under 50 has risen approximately 2–3% per year since the early 1990s.
Global picture
1 in 10 new colorectal cancer diagnoses globally are now in people under 50 ‚Äî a figure that has roughly doubled since the 1990s.
Australia specifically
Around 15,500 Australians are diagnosed with bowel cancer each year. It kills more Australians under 50 than any other cancer.
The consistent finding: researchers point to two dietary factors driving this ‚Äî a rise in ultra-processed food consumption and a sharp decline in dietary fibre.

What is dietary fibre?
Definition
Dietary fibre is a type of carbohydrate found in plant foods that the body cannot fully digest. Instead of being absorbed into the bloodstream, it passes largely intact through the digestive system ‚Äî feeding beneficial gut bacteria and supporting bowel health along the way.
Two main types
Soluble fibre ‚Äî dissolves in water; slows digestion, helps manage blood sugar and cholesterol. Found in oats, legumes, and fruit.

Insoluble fibre ‚Äî does not dissolve; adds bulk and helps material move through the gut. Found in wholegrain bread, vegetables, and nuts.
Recommended daily intake
25–30g per day
What most Australians actually consume
15–20g per day
Teenagers typically get even less than this.
Why it matters here: fibre feeds the gut microbiome, reduces inflammation in the bowel lining, and is one of the strongest dietary predictors of colorectal cancer risk.

Making it look good.
Fast food companies spend enormous amounts on food styling and photography. The goal isn't to show you what the food actually looks like ‚Äî it's to trigger desire before you think critically about the choice.
Visual distraction
Burgers are almost always photographed with vivid green lettuce, bright red tomato, and fresh garnishes. This pulls your eye toward colour and freshness, away from the grey or brown processed component in the middle.
Colour psychology
Red and yellow are used heavily across fast food brands ‚Äî they stimulate appetite and urgency. Warm lighting hides oil sheen and makes chips appear crispier than they are.
Portion deception
Products are shot at flattering angles with props chosen to make portions look larger. Food stylists routinely use non-food materials ‚Äî motor oil instead of syrup, hairspray on fruit ‚Äî to maintain "perfect" appearances under studio lighting.
So what? If a product needs this much effort just to look appealing, what does that tell us about the design problem we're actually trying to solve in a food app?

The Goldilocks Rule
I Do
Too Broad
"How might we make healthy eating better for everyone?"
No specific user, no context, no traction ‚Äî could mean anything.
Just Right
"How might we help students who are in a rush feel confident a healthy choice won't slow them down?"
Specific user + situation, clear friction point, solution completely open.
Too Narrow
"How might we add a calorie filter for items under 400kJ to the app?"
Already a solution ‚Äî the design thinking is over before it began.
Look for: specific user  ¬∑  a friction point or desire  ¬∑  NO solution hidden inside the question

The Sentence Frame
Write it this way.
How Might We ‚Äî Template
How might we help [specific user / situation]
[do / feel / avoid something]
so that [outcome]?
Watch the outcome: it should describe a result for the user, not prescribe what the solution looks like. If it sounds like a feature, pull back.
